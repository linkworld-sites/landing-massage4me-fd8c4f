But the question is still worth answering properly, because frequency matters. A single session a year won't build the cumulative relief that regular sessions do. Here's what I tell clients when they ask.

## What Massage Actually Does to Stress

Before we get to frequency, it helps to understand the mechanism — because this changes how you think about timing.

Stress is physiological before it's psychological. The moment your threat-detection system activates, your body releases cortisol and adrenaline, increases heart rate, tightens muscles, and shifts blood flow to your extremities. This is useful when you're outrunning something. It's less useful when the threat is your inbox.

Chronic stress means this system runs on low-level activation almost constantly. The result is elevated baseline cortisol, a persistently elevated heart rate, chronically shortened muscles (particularly in the shoulders, neck, and lower back), disrupted sleep, and a generalised sense of being wound tight.

Therapeutic massage — particularly Swedish relaxation — works on this system directly. It:

- **Reduces cortisol measurably** — multiple studies have found cortisol levels drop significantly after a 45–60 minute session
- **Increases serotonin and dopamine** — the same neurotransmitters that antidepressants target, produced endogenously through touch
- **Activates the parasympathetic nervous system** — your "rest and digest" mode, the counterpart to the stress response
- **Releases muscular holding patterns** — the physical architecture of chronic stress, which perpetuates the nervous system activation even when the stressor is gone

This is why clients often describe feeling not just relaxed but *reset* — like something that's been wound tight has finally been allowed to unwind.

## So How Often, Exactly?

There's no single answer. But here's a framework that applies to most people:

### For Acute or High-Stress Periods — Weekly or Fortnightly

If you're in a genuinely high-stress period — a major project, a life transition, an ongoing difficult situation — weekly sessions produce the most sustained relief. You're essentially keeping the cortisol cycle from building back to its accumulated state between sessions.

Fortnightly (every two weeks) is the practical compromise for most working professionals: frequent enough to build cumulative effect, sustainable enough to maintain over months.

At Massage4me, we see many clients who book fortnightly as their standard cadence during demanding professional periods, switching to monthly when things quieten. The same-day availability means when a particularly brutal week arrives, you can respond to it immediately rather than waiting for a slot that's two weeks out.

### For Maintenance and General Wellbeing — Monthly

Once-a-month massage is the most common cadence for people who are generally managing stress well but want to stay ahead of it. It's enough to maintain the physiological baseline without feeling like a significant time commitment.

Think of it less as treatment and more as maintenance — the equivalent of regular exercise for the cardiovascular system, applied to the nervous system.

### For Recovery from Burnout — Intensive Short Course

If you're recovering from genuine burnout — not just tired, but the kind of exhausted that feels structural — a short intensive course of 3–4 sessions over 3–4 weeks, followed by a monthly maintenance schedule, is often the most effective approach.

The initial intensive course breaks the accumulated patterns; the maintenance schedule prevents them from returning.

## Which Type of Massage Works Best for Stress?

For stress relief specifically, these are the treatments that consistently produce the strongest results:

### Swedish Relaxation Massage

Long, flowing effleurage strokes, moderate pressure, systematic progression through the body. The Swedish session is precisely calibrated for nervous system regulation — it's what most people picture when they think of massage, and what the majority of clinical research on massage and stress uses as the intervention.

A 60-minute Swedish session is the standard stress-relief benchmark. For people with significant accumulated tension, 90 minutes allows the full body to be addressed without rushing, and typically produces noticeably deeper relaxation.

### Aromatherapy Massage

Swedish technique combined with essential oils selected for their specific effects on the nervous system. Lavender, bergamot, and frankincense have documented anxiolytic (anxiety-reducing) effects when inhaled during massage. For people whose stress has a strong emotional or mood component, aromatherapy massage adds a meaningful layer to the purely physical work.

Available in 60, 90, and 120-minute sessions at Massage4me.

### Hot Stone Ritual

Warm basalt stones melt deep tension through both heat and weight — the heat penetrates farther than hands alone, reaching muscular layers that take longer to release through pressure. For people who find it difficult to fully relax during massage (a common stress-related phenomenon — your nervous system is on alert even when you're trying to unwind), the warmth of the stones often bypasses this resistance.

Available in 90 and 120-minute sessions.

## Building a Rhythm That Works for You

The most important frequency is the one you'll actually maintain. A monthly session you reliably keep is worth more than a fortnightly intention you half-fulfil.

A few things that help:

**Book the next session before you leave the current one.** The research on habit formation is clear: intention plus scheduling produces follow-through; intention alone often doesn't.

**Use your body as a guide.** When you notice the tension returning — the jaw clenching, the shoulders climbing toward the ears, the lower back tightening — that's your body's request, not your imagination.

## A Note on What Regular Massage Is (and Isn't)

Regular massage for stress relief isn't a substitute for addressing the sources of your stress. If your job is genuinely unsustainable, or a relationship is causing real harm, massage will help you manage the physiological toll — but it's not a solution to the underlying situation.

What it is: a reliable, evidence-based tool for maintaining your baseline, lowering your stress floor, and ensuring that your body doesn't become a place where tension lives permanently. That matters. Because a nervous system that isn't constantly in repair mode has more capacity for everything else.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the minimum number of sessions to feel a difference?**
Most people feel a significant difference after their first session. A lasting, cumulative shift typically begins to appear after 3–4 sessions. If your stress is chronic, it has taken time to build — expect the resolution to take a few sessions too.

**Is there such a thing as too much massage?**
Yes, in practice, though the threshold is higher than most people reach. Daily deep tissue work over extended periods can lead to tissue inflammation. For stress-relief massage (Swedish, aromatherapy, hot stone), 2–3 times per week is generally the practical upper limit, and most people find once a week more than sufficient.

**Does massage help with anxiety as well as stress?**
Yes. The physiological overlap between stress and anxiety is significant, and massage addresses both through the same mechanisms — cortisol reduction, parasympathetic activation, and muscular release. Multiple clinical trials have found regular massage reduces anxiety symptoms across a range of clinical presentations.

**Can I get a massage during a particularly acute stress response — like the day of a big presentation?**
Yes. A 60-minute session in the hours before a high-stakes event can significantly reduce the physical manifestation of anxiety — heart rate, muscle tension, cognitive load. Our same-day booking makes this genuinely practical rather than aspirational.

**Will I need to talk during the session?**
Only as much as you want to. Most therapists ask a few initial questions to understand your presentation. After that, the session is yours — silence is not just acceptable, it's often the most effective context for the nervous system to fully release.
